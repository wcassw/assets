# Daniel 9 in the KJV and Jewish Tanakh: Explanation, Summary, and Dates

## First correction: Daniel is not in the Torah

In Judaism, **Torah** usually means the Five Books of Moses: Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy.

**Daniel is not in the Torah.** Daniel is part of the **Tanakh**, but in the Jewish order it is placed in **Ketuvim / Writings**, not in the Prophets.

So the better question is:

> What does **Daniel 9** say in the KJV, and how is it read in the **Jewish Tanakh/tradition**?

---

# 1. What Daniel 9 says in the KJV

Daniel 9 has three main parts.

## A. Daniel reads Jeremiah’s prophecy of 70 years

Daniel says this happened in:

> “the first year of Darius the son of Ahasuerus” — Daniel 9:1 KJV

Then Daniel understands from “the books” that Jeremiah had spoken of **70 years** for Jerusalem’s desolations.

This is the first major date or time period in the chapter.

| Daniel 9 reference | Time marker |
|---|---:|
| Daniel 9:1 | First year of Darius |
| Daniel 9:2 | 70 years of Jerusalem’s desolation |

Historically, the fall of Babylon is usually dated to **539 BCE**, when Cyrus the Great conquered Babylon. However, the identity of “Darius the Mede” is debated, so exact modern dating of Daniel 9:1 is not simple.

---

## B. Daniel prays a national confession

Daniel does not begin by making a prediction. He begins by confessing sin.

His prayer says, in summary:

- God is righteous.
- Israel has sinned.
- The people ignored the prophets.
- The exile came because Israel broke God’s law.
- Daniel asks God to forgive and restore Jerusalem.

A key KJV line is:

> “O Lord, hear; O Lord, forgive; O Lord, hearken and do; defer not…” — Daniel 9:19 KJV

The focus is **repentance before restoration**.

---

## C. Gabriel explains the “seventy weeks”

The most famous part is Daniel 9:24–27. In the KJV, Gabriel says:

> “Seventy weeks are determined upon thy people and upon thy holy city…” — Daniel 9:24 KJV

The Jewish Chabad Tanakh translation renders this as:

> “Seventy weeks [of years] have been decreed upon your people and upon the city of your Sanctuary…”

That bracketed phrase is important: **“weeks” means sevens**, and Jewish interpretation commonly reads this as **weeks of years**.

So:

**70 weeks = 70 sevens = 490 years**

Chabad/Rashi explains the seventy weeks as **490 years**, made up of:

| Period | Years |
|---|---:|
| Babylonian exile | 70 |
| Second Temple period | 420 |
| Total | 490 |

---

# 2. The date/time structure in Daniel 9

Daniel 9 gives this sequence:

| Text | Time period | Meaning |
|---|---:|---|
| Daniel 9:2 | 70 years | Jerusalem’s desolation, linked to Jeremiah |
| Daniel 9:24 | 70 weeks/sevens | Commonly read as 490 years |
| Daniel 9:25 | 7 weeks | First segment |
| Daniel 9:25 | 62 weeks | Second segment |
| Daniel 9:26 | After the 62 weeks | An anointed one is cut off; city/sanctuary destroyed |
| Daniel 9:27 | 1 week | Final segment |
| Daniel 9:27 | Middle of the week | Sacrifice and offering cease |

The total is:

**7 weeks + 62 weeks + 1 week = 70 weeks**

Or:

**49 years + 434 years + 7 years = 490 years**

---

# 3. What the Jewish Tanakh says

The Jewish Bible text is very close in meaning, but the translation and interpretation differ in key places.

## “Messiah” / “anointed one”

The KJV says:

> “unto the Messiah the Prince” — Daniel 9:25 KJV

The Jewish Chabad translation says:

> “until [the] anointed king” — Daniel 9:25

This matters because the Hebrew word **mashiach** means **anointed one**.

In Jewish reading, it does not automatically mean “Jesus” or the later Christian title “the Messiah” in every occurrence. Kings, priests, and even Cyrus can be called “anointed” in biblical language.

Rashi identifies the “anointed king” in Daniel 9:25 with **Cyrus, king of Persia**, because Isaiah 45:1 calls Cyrus God’s anointed.

---

## Rashi’s Jewish interpretation

Rashi reads the “seventy weeks” as a timeline from the **destruction of the First Temple** to the **destruction of the Second Temple**.

In this reading:

- the 70 weeks are **490 years**,
- the timeline begins around the First Temple destruction,
- the timeline moves toward the Second Temple destruction,
- the 70 years of Babylonian exile are included,
- the Second Temple period is counted as 420 years.

Rashi’s calculation:

| Period | Years |
|---|---:|
| Babylonian exile | 70 |
| Second Temple stood | 420 |
| Total | 490 |

This is a classic Jewish framing of the passage.

---

# 4. What the Torah says related to this theme

Again, **Daniel 9 itself is not in the Torah**. But the Torah does contain themes that Daniel 9 uses.

## A. Exile because of covenant disobedience

Leviticus 26 warns that if Israel breaks the covenant, the land will become desolate and the people will be scattered among the nations.

This connects strongly with Daniel 9 because Daniel confesses that the exile happened because Israel sinned and ignored God’s law.

---

## B. The land enjoying its Sabbaths

Leviticus 26 also says the land would “enjoy its Sabbaths” during the desolation.

That helps explain why Daniel is thinking in terms of:

- years,
- Sabbath cycles,
- sevens,
- judgment,
- restoration.

---

## C. Repentance and restoration

Deuteronomy 30 says that when Israel returns to God, God will restore them and gather them from the nations.

That is exactly the spirit of Daniel 9: Daniel confesses sin and asks God to restore Jerusalem.

---

# 5. Main difference between Christian and Jewish readings

## Christian reading, especially traditional Christian reading

Many Christians read Daniel 9:24–27 as a prophecy about:

- the coming of the Messiah,
- the death of the Messiah,
- the destruction of Jerusalem,
- and sometimes a final end-times period.

This reading often focuses on the KJV phrases:

> “Messiah the Prince”

and:

> “Messiah shall be cut off”

---

## Jewish reading

The Jewish reading does not usually treat Daniel 9 as a prediction of Jesus.

In Rashi’s reading:

- the 70 weeks are **490 years**,
- the timeline runs from the First Temple destruction toward the Second Temple destruction,
- the “anointed” figure in Daniel 9:25 is connected with **Cyrus**,
- the later destruction is connected with **Titus and Rome**.

So the two traditions read the same chapter but place the timeline differently.

---

# 6. Summary

Daniel 9 says Daniel realized Jeremiah’s **70 years** of Jerusalem’s desolation were significant.

He prayed a deep confession for Israel’s sins and asked God to restore Jerusalem.

Gabriel then gave him a larger time prophecy: **70 weeks**, commonly understood as **70 sevens of years**, or **490 years**.

The Jewish Torah does **not** contain Daniel 9, because Daniel is not part of the Torah. In Judaism, Daniel is in the **Writings / Ketuvim**. But the Torah contains the background ideas behind Daniel 9:

- exile for covenant disobedience,
- the land resting during desolation,
- repentance,
- eventual restoration.
